She Actually Did It

Now What: What Nobody Tells You About After

By Kimberly

I am a goal oriented person.

Always have been. You put a target in front of me, I will build a plan, work the plan, hit the mark, and be looking for the next thing before most people have finished celebrating. Twenty three years of program management will do that to you. It is just how I move.

So getting to Madrid was not the hard part. I want to be honest about that. The decision, the paperwork, the flight, landing in a new country with my dog and my whole life reorganized into boxes. I did all of that. Goal achieved.

And then I sat down in a rented apartment and thought: okay. Now what.

Not what if I made a mistake. Not I want to go home. Just the clean, quiet, completely unfamiliar feeling of having no next thing to reach for.

I had rented furniture before I arrived because I did not want to land right before Christmas in an empty apartment. It was functional. It looked exactly like what it was. Rented furniture in a residential neighborhood in a city where I did not know anyone, did not speak the language, and had no contract coming in. The walls were fine. The couch was fine. None of it felt like mine.

Monday looked like Tuesday. Tuesday looked like Wednesday. Every day had the same shape, which is to say it had almost no shape at all. I was still figuring out that you cannot buy four or five days worth of groceries at a time in Europe because the food will not last. So even that small routine kept resetting on me.

The only reason I left the apartment most days was Chloe.

She had to be walked three, four times a day, so I went outside because she needed me to. She was not settling in either. She was laying next to her carrier every single day, waiting to go home. Neither of us fully understood what we had gotten into.

I would walk her to the park and watch her run. The dog parents would stand around and talk the way dog parents do everywhere, easy and comfortable, held together by the common language of their animals. I stood off to the side. Not because I did not want to be in it. Because I did not have the words yet. So I watched from the edge of a moment I could not quite enter.

Sometimes I would walk to the mall just to have somewhere to go. It was about twenty five minutes each way. I would get there, walk around, walk back. Not shopping. Just moving. Just giving the day somewhere to put itself.

Nobody at the grocery store spoke English. Nobody at the mall did either. But I went anyway because routine was the only architecture I had.

And then at the end of all of it, at nine or ten o'clock at night my time, I could finally call someone. My people were six hours behind me. Sometimes seven for the family in central time. Which means all day, every day, I was waiting. Their morning was my afternoon. Their lunch was my evening. By the time they were free, I had already lived an entire day alone and was just trying to hold on until I could hear a familiar voice.

One of those nights I finally got my cousin on the phone. She asked how I was doing. I told her I was alright. And then I said: but I am sitting here like, now what.

Because that was the truth of it. I am a person who has always been doing something. Always taking care of somebody. Always in a class, working on a business, handling something for someone. Busy was my baseline. And now I was sitting in a quiet residential neighborhood with rented furniture and a dog who missed home and not a single thing that needed me.

*   *   *

Here is what I held onto.

Everything had fallen into place to get me here. The paperwork. The relocation company. The timing. All of it had lined up in ways that were too specific to be coincidence. I had prayed on that. And when the contract fell through with no income and no visible safety net, I prayed on that too. I said I was going to stand on faith. I meant it. And I wanted the Lord to know I meant it.

So the quiet apartment was not me doubting. The rented furniture and the solo mall walks and the waiting until nine pm to talk to somebody, that was me actually doing the thing I said I was going to do.

Standing on faith does not always look like peace. Sometimes it looks like a long Wednesday with nowhere to be and a dog who wants to go home and a phone that cannot ring for six more hours.

The shift came about a month in when Spanish class started. I came in with almost zero Spanish. Survival Spanish, the kind you need at airports and nowhere else. A1 level meant I still could not understand anything. The city still sounded like people talking fast in a language I did not know, because they were. But there were other people in that room. People on digital nomad visas. People who had also arrived somewhere new and were also figuring it out. We were all sitting in the same confusion.

I would not call them friends yet. They were class associates. But they were people. And that was the first thread.

*   *   *

If you are someone who moves by goals, and you are somewhere in the middle of building toward something big, I want you to know this part is coming. The achievement. The arrival. And then the silence that follows when the goal is done and the life has not filled in yet.

Nobody warns you about the rented furniture. Nobody tells you about waiting all day just to hear a familiar voice. Nobody mentions that standing on faith has a Tuesday afternoon in it where nothing is happening and you just have to stay.

You are not doing it wrong.

It just takes a minute. And then one day you have class associates. And that is enough to start.
