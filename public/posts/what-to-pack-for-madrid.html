What to Pack for Madrid: A Real Style and Practical Guide

What actually earns space in your suitcase for Madrid's seasons and streets.

By Kimberly  ·  May 2026  ·  Madrid, Spain

Madrid Packing and Prep Before You Go

Let me tell you something nobody puts in a packing guide.

I arrived in Madrid in December. I had four checked bags weighing 65 pounds each, a carry on roller, a shoulder bag the size of a small country, and a very strong opinion about how prepared I was. I had packed by category. Seasonally organized. Color coded. It was a whole system.

What I did not pack was a pair of in between shoes.

Not winter boots. Not gym shoes. Not dress shoes. The shoes you wear when it is not quite cold enough for boots but too cool for sandals. The transitional shoes. The ones you need approximately every other day in Madrid. Those shoes. Left behind in Atlanta having absolutely no idea how much they would be missed.

Spain had not changed. I was the one adapting. In boots. In April.

Learn from my experience. Here is the real packing guide for Madrid, by season, by situation, and by someone who has actually lived through all of it.

First, Understand Madrid's Weather Because It Will Surprise You

Madrid has four genuine seasons but they do not always behave the way you expect them to. The city sits on a high plateau which means the temperature swings between morning and evening can catch you completely off guard. You can leave the house in a light jacket feeling perfectly reasonable and spend the afternoon questioning every decision you made that morning.

Winter: December through February

Average temperatures 4 to 10 degrees Celsius, 39 to 50 degrees Fahrenheit. Cool to cold especially in the mornings and evenings. Wind can make it feel significantly colder. Snow is possible but rare. I have seen it snow maybe once since arriving in December and it was not the dramatic winter wonderland I had mentally prepared for. Pack a proper coat, layers, scarves, your beanie, warm socks, and boots.

Spring: March through May

Average temperatures 10 to 20 degrees Celsius, 50 to 68 degrees Fahrenheit. This is where Madrid gets tricky. Spring mornings can be genuinely cold. Afternoons can be warm and lovely. Evenings dip back down again. You will wear three different outfit configurations in one day if you are not prepared. Light layers are everything. A light to medium jacket, scarves, your beanie for mornings and evenings, and transitional shoes. If you are visiting in April specifically, do not let anyone tell you it is warm enough to leave the scarf at home. It is not.

Summer: June through August

Average temperatures 22 to 35 degrees Celsius, 72 to 95 degrees Fahrenheit. Hot. Genuinely hot. July and August especially can hit temperatures that are not a joke. Pack light breathable clothing, comfortable walking shoes, sunscreen, sunglasses, and a light layer for air conditioned restaurants and museums which are aggressively cold inside regardless of what it is doing outside.

Fall: September through November

Average temperatures 12 to 22 degrees Celsius, 54 to 72 degrees Fahrenheit. One of the best times to visit Madrid. Pleasant temperatures, fewer tourists, and the city feels alive in a really wonderful way. Same strategy as spring. Layers. Scarf. The beanie by October. A medium weight jacket.

The One Item I Wear Almost Every Single Day

Let us talk about the hat.

Not a heavy winter hat. Not a baseball cap. A soft warm slouchy knit beanie, the kind that is not too structured, not too thick, folds into your bag when you go inside, does not completely destroy your hair, and works with everything from a casual walk to a dinner out.

I have worn mine almost every single day since I arrived in December. It has saved me from unexpected rain. It has kept me warm when I wore a thinner jacket than the morning temperature deserved. It has been the one item I consistently reach for when walking out the door in any season except the height of summer.

I only just started leaving home without it and it is April.

The key is the weight. It is not a ski hat. It is not so heavy that you overheat on the metro or feel ridiculous indoors. It is that perfect in between weight that works across seasons and situations. Think soft knit, slightly slouchy, fits close enough to stay on in wind but relaxed enough to not feel like you are wearing a helmet.

The Body Heat Strategy Nobody Talks About

Here is something practical that changed how I dress for Madrid.

Keep your head warm and keep your feet warm and your body will do the rest. When your extremities are covered your body retains heat more efficiently which means you can wear lighter layers and still be comfortable.

In practice this means a medium weight jacket plus a scarf plus your beanie equals warmth without bulk. You do not need the heaviest coat in your closet for most of Madrid's winter. You can move freely on the metro, in and out of restaurants, through markets, without feeling like you are carrying your entire wardrobe on your body.

What to Actually Pack: The Real List

Shoes

Comfortable walking shoes are non negotiable. You will walk more than you expect and some of it is uphill. I wear my Hokas and they have been excellent. Whatever your version of a comfortable supportive walking shoe is, bring it and wear it onto the plane so it does not take up suitcase space.

In between shoes. This is the gap in most people's packing. Something that is not a gym shoe and not a formal shoe. A clean sneaker, a loafer, a casual flat. Something you can wear to a nicer café or a casual dinner without feeling underdressed.

Boots or warm weather shoes depending on your season. If you are coming in winter or fall, boots. If you are coming in summer, comfortable sandals that can handle cobblestones.

Leave the stilettos at home. Cobblestone streets are everywhere in Madrid's historic areas. Heels and cobblestones have a complicated relationship that never ends well for the heels.

Clothing

Layers, layers, layers. This applies to every season except peak summer. The ability to add or remove a layer is worth more than any single heavy piece.

A scarf. At least one. Possibly two. It works for warmth, for style, and for covering your shoulders in churches.

A light to medium jacket. Even in summer for air conditioned spaces and cool evenings.

Comfortable everyday clothes. Madrid leans stylish but not formal for everyday life. Clean, put together, and comfortable covers most situations.

Something slightly dressier. For nicer dinners or nights out. You do not need a lot but you want at least one option.

For Longer Stays Specifically

Your own pillow. Sleep matters. Hotel pillows are a gamble. Your own pillow is a known quantity and worth the suitcase space for an extended stay.

A door organizer, five tier. European bathrooms frequently have little to no cabinet storage. A door organizer changes your bathroom situation completely.

Shout Wipes. Bring them from home. They are hard to find in Spain and you will need them. The orange and blue packaging. You know the ones.

Your neti pot. The Spanish version is not the same. If you use one regularly bring yours from home.

Supplements, but not too many. I brought an entire pharmacy worth of supplements. I used maybe a third of them. Be realistic about what you actually take consistently versus what you think you will take when you are living your best life abroad.

A Personal Note

I packed for the woman I imagined I would be in Madrid. The one who took every supplement every day, who did the full skincare routine every night, who used the neti pot consistently. The real me unpacked a lot of that and quietly admitted she was never going to use half of it.

Pack for the person you actually are. Not the one you keep promising you will become.

Tech and Practical Essentials

Two portable charging banks. Not one. Two. You will use them both.

A multi plug charging bank. One device that charges your Android, your iPhone, and your iPad without carrying three separate cables.

Plug adapter. Spain uses Type C and F plugs at 220V. Buy before you leave.

Portable WiFi device. Essential for longer stays as a bridge until your home WiFi is set up.

Airplane blanket. Keep it. Always keep it. It has more uses than you think.

Scissors. In your checked bag obviously. But bring them. You will need scissors at some point and you will not think to pack them until it is 11 PM on a Sunday.

A pen and a small notebook. Physical paper still matters. Customs forms, addresses, notes about things you want to remember.

A measuring tape with both inches AND centimeters. If you are staying longer and doing any furniture arranging or shopping, you need a measuring tape that speaks metric. An inches only tape measure in a European apartment is a lesson in humility. Boo on that.

Download Before You Fly

Netflix shows, movies, podcasts, anything you want to watch or listen to on the plane or in your first few days before your home WiFi is set up. Offline Google Maps for Madrid. All essential apps fully set up with payment verified.

Short Term Visit Versus Long Term Stay: Pack Differently

If you are visiting for one to two weeks, think vacation. What do you actually need for the days you are there? A week's worth of outfits, your comfortable shoes, your scarf and hat depending on season, your tech essentials, and your documents.

If you are staying for a month or longer, be strategic. Pack what you use consistently, not what you think you might use someday. The things that are genuinely worth bringing from home for a longer stay are the health and wellness items that do not have a direct Spanish equivalent. Everything else you can figure out when you get here. Possibly with the help of an AI tool and a picture of the grocery store aisle. Just saying.

One Last Thing: The Glovo App

Download Glovo before you leave home and have it set up with payment ready. Glovo is a delivery app that delivers from restaurants, grocery stores, pharmacies, and regular shops. Not just food. Actual items you need. A heater for example. If you arrive and find yourself without heat on a Sunday three days before Christmas, Glovo is how you solve that problem at whatever hour it presents itself. It is not just a convenience app. For the first week of a longer stay especially, before you know where everything is, Glovo is a lifeline.

What Other Guides Do Not Tell You

The Metro Outfit Problem Madrid is a metro city. You are going in and out of heated underground stations, cold outdoor platforms, warm restaurants, and breezy evening streets all in the same day. Pack layers you can actually add and remove quickly without making a scene on a crowded metro car.
The UV Index Will Humble You Madrid sits on a high plateau at significant elevation. The UV index here is higher than most visitors expect including in winter. Bring sunscreen from home. It exists here but it is more expensive than you expect.
The Air Conditioning Paradox of Summer Madrid summers are hot. Then you walk into a restaurant and the air conditioning is set to a temperature that suggests the management has never actually been warm. Pack a light layer in your day bag every single day in summer.
The Cobblestone Shoe Truth Thin soled flats, certain sandal styles, anything without actual cushioning or grip, will make you miserable on Madrid's historic streets within two hours. Cute and comfortable are not mutually exclusive. Find shoes that are both.
The Pharmacy Is Your Friend and Your Wallet Will Thank You Skincare brands like Avene, La Roche-Posay, Bioderma, and Caudalie are significantly cheaper in Spanish pharmacies than in the US or UK. Save the suitcase space and treat yourself at the farmacia when you arrive.
Period Products, Let Us Just Say It Tampon options in Spain are more limited than what most American women are used to. The variety is smaller and certain specific products you may rely on are simply not available here. Pack enough from home or be prepared to adjust.
Earplugs Are Not Optional If You Value Sleep Madrid is loud at night. It is loud at 2 AM. And 3 AM. If you are a light sleeper, earplugs are not a nice to have. They are infrastructure.
The Laundry Situation for Longer Stays European washing machines run longer cycles. Many Madrid apartments do not have dryers. In winter, things take longer to dry than you expect. Pack enough of your essential items to rotate while other things are drying.
The Reusable Water Bottle Is Not Just an Eco Choice Madrid's tap water is safe and good. But the city is dry especially in summer. A reusable water bottle means you can fill up anywhere without paying every time you need water.
La Calima: Saharan Dust Nobody Warns You About Spain is periodically affected by a weather phenomenon where fine dust and sand particles blow north from the Sahara Desert. It is most common between February and June. The critical thing to know is that you cannot always tell when it is happening just by looking at the sky. The sky can be perfectly clear and beautiful while the invisible particles are already irritating your eyes. This happened on a completely gorgeous day with no visible haze. Pack a pair of glasses, even non-prescription clear lenses, to shield your eyes outdoors. If your eyes become irritated, head to the nearest Farmacia and ask for lagrimas artificiales, which are artificial tears. Check real time calima conditions at calimacanarias.com.
