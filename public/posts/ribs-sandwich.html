I Walked an Hour for Ribs and Christmas Eve Dinner Was a Sorry Ham Sandwich

By Kimberly  ·  December 24, 2024  ·  7 min read

This was not how Christmas Eve was supposed to go.

I'm the one who organizes the family Zoom every year. Secret Santa, the whole thing. My cousin picks who has who. But everything else? That's me. I collect everyone's three gift options, build the list, send it out. I even put my uncle on payroll, unofficially, since he's retired and has time. His job is to chase down the stragglers who don't meet the deadline. Every year. I run this thing like a production.

This year I'm running it from Madrid.

The call was set for 5:30 Eastern. 11:30 my time. Fine. I can do that.

But first I needed to eat.

It's Christmas Eve and I wanted something that felt like home. I found a spot online with ribs, wings, all of it. Ordered everything. No drivers available. And the independent woman in me said, it's only a 28 minute walk. I'll just go get it.

Y'all.

I pack both phones and my power bank because I am not the one who lets her phone die. I bundle up and head out feeling confident. Six days walking these streets every single day. I thought I had a general idea of where I was going.

I did not.

I went the wrong way entirely. Had to stop, reroute, which added 12 minutes to a trip that was already 28 minutes. Fine. I keep going. What nobody had mentioned about Madrid is that this city is uphill. Mostly uphill. It's cold. Dark since 5:30. And I am out here trekking.

I finally get to the area and I cannot find this place. I'm walking to the address and back. And back again. More than a dozen times. Because it has to be here somewhere. The app is still sending me alerts that my order is ready. Ready where? Because what is at this address is an apartment building. Attached to a restaurant. That is very much closed.

I take a picture. Not for memories. For Uber Eats. Because I want my 70 euros back and I need proof that I showed up and there was nowhere to pick up a single rib.

I finally give up. Walk another five minutes until I find a well lit area, call a rideshare, and get in the car hungry, frustrated, and cold.

As soon as I get in, my best friend calls.

She had just talked to my daughter. And I complain the entire ride home. Everything. The wrong turn. The uphill. The apartment building. The closed restaurant. The alerts. The 70 euros. All of it. Ten minutes of pure debrief.

Then she says, I wanted to catch you before your family call.

My family call. The one I organized. The one I had completely forgotten about because I had been lost in the streets of Madrid for the better part of an hour on Christmas Eve.

I get upstairs, pour a glass of red wine immediately because I need a full personality reset, and make myself a sandwich. I eat while we're still on the phone. She stays on with me right up until it's time.

I get on the Zoom. Everyone is there. We're laughing, catching up, doing what we do. And then some of the family members mention they're hungry. We hadn't gotten to gift giving yet. Someone suggests postponing it 45 minutes.

I do the math out loud. That's 12:30, maybe 12:45 my time.

I let them know I would not be on that call. I made my uncle the host and I dropped off.

I wasn't angry. I want to be clear about that. I understood. People were hungry. Life happens. But I was disappointed. Quietly, in that way you don't really say out loud because you're the one who chose to move six hours away and so you just absorb it and keep it moving.

I sat with it for a while. And then, about 30 minutes later, I quietly jumped back on the Zoom. Not to participate. Just to be in the room. Everyone was eating, talking, doing what families do on Christmas Eve. Nobody saw me.

I watched for a little while. Then I closed the laptop and went to bed.

Here's what nobody tells you when you're planning your big bold move.

You can do everything right. You can organize the calls, employ your uncle, stay six hours ahead and still show up. And some years, Christmas Eve is just going to be a sandwich.

Not because anyone did anything wrong. Not because your family doesn't love you. But because you left. And leaving means sometimes you miss things. Not the big dramatic things. The quiet ones. The ones that only hurt a little but hurt in a very specific place.

If you're thinking about making a move like this, I need you to know that part too. The city is beautiful. The life is real. And some nights you're going to eat a sandwich alone at midnight and feel every mile between you and the people you love.

That's the part nobody puts in the caption.

Spain has not changed. I am the one adapting.
