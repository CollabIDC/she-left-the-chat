Madrid Travel Guides Before You Go

There are a thousand Madrid travel guides on the internet. Most of them were written by someone who spent a long weekend there, ate at the most Instagrammable spots, took approximately 400 photos of churros, and called themselves an expert.

This is not that guide.

I actually live here. I packed up my life, booked a one way ticket, and moved to Madrid because I always wanted to experience living abroad. How long am I staying? Honestly, I have no idea. Ask me after a couple of tinto de veranos. That is the local version of a sangria and yes, it is exactly as dangerous to your decision making as it sounds.

What I can tell you is that Madrid has a way of making you forget you ever had a plan. And I say that as a program manager, corporate trainer, and AI consultant who builds plans for a living. Color coded, perfectly timed, logically sequenced plans. Madrid looked at my plans, smiled politely, and then told me Thursday was actually Jueves Santo, a national holiday, and everything was closed. Google had not mentioned this. My color coded calendar had not mentioned this. Madrid did not feel the need to mention this in advance either.

Spain has not changed. I am the one adapting.

That sentence right there is the whole thesis of She Left the Chat. I did not come here to critique or compare. I came here to experience, to learn, to figure it out, and to bring you along for the ride. Some days that looks like discovering the most beautiful park you have ever seen in your life. Other days it looks like standing in a grocery store aisle having a full consultation with my AI tool trying to locate the butter while my dog Chloe waits at home with absolutely zero sympathy.

Speaking of Chloe. She is here too. She has opinions about every park in Madrid. We will get to that.

Why Madrid Though

Let me be honest with you about something. Madrid does not get the hype that Barcelona gets. Tourists flock to Barcelona for the beaches and the Gaudí architecture and the content. Madrid just sits there being extraordinary while nobody pays attention.

And honestly? That is part of what makes it so good.

Madrid rewards the people who actually show up for it. It is walkable in a way that surprises most visitors. It is affordable in a way that will make you question every expensive city you have ever lived in. It has world class art, food that will genuinely introduce you to flavors you did not know you were missing, and a pace of life that makes you rethink everything you thought your schedule needed to look like.

The people are warm. The coffee costs €1.50 and tastes like it was made by someone who actually cares. The parks are the kind of green that makes you want to sit on a bench and read an actual book like it is 1987. And the food. We are going to talk about the food a lot throughout this series. Consider yourself warned.

There is also the small matter of the hills. Nobody puts the hills in the travel guides. The photos show charming cobblestone streets and romantic staircases and it all looks very lovely. What the photos do not show is what your face looks like at the top. Comfortable shoes are not a suggestion here. They are a survival strategy. We will talk about this more in the packing guide.

Madrid did not come to fit my plan. It came to rearrange it.

A Few Things Nobody Told Me Before I Got Here

Before we get into the detailed guides, here are the things I wish someone had just said to me straight:

The Food Schedule Is Real And It Will Mess With You

Lunch is at 2 PM. Dinner is at 9 PM or later. Show up to a restaurant at 6 PM looking for dinner and you will be met with genuine confusion and possibly an empty dining room. Back home, 9 PM was pajamas and a rerun of something comfortable. In Madrid 9 PM is when the restaurant starts to fill up. I am not saying one approach is wrong. I am saying I had to buy new pajamas. Adjust your schedule and you will have one of the best eating experiences of your life.

Not Every Restaurant Does Takeaway

This one surprises almost every visitor. In Madrid eating is a sit down social experience. Many traditional restaurants and bars simply do not offer takeaway. If you want to ask, the phrase is para llevar which means to take away. But ask before you order so you are not caught off guard if the answer is no.

Madrid Is Incredibly Walkable But Some Of It Is Uphill

Significantly uphill in certain areas. I am a logical person. I plan routes. I factor in distance. What I did not factor in was the elevation change between where I was standing and where I needed to be. The walk was further than anticipated and parts of it were straight up. Comfortable shoes are non negotiable. We will talk about this properly in the packing guide.

The Metro Is Your Best Friend

Clean, fast, affordable, and easy to use even if you do not speak a word of Spanish. There is an entire post dedicated to it. Read it before you land.

Tipping Is Not What You Are Used To

If you are coming from the US the instinct to tip 20% on everything is going to kick in immediately. Resist it. A euro or two for genuinely great service is considered generous here. Nobody is watching you leave and calculating a percentage. That is just not how it works. Full details in the culture guide.

Bring A Reusable Bag Everywhere

Every single shop in Spain charges for plastic bags. Every one. You will learn this lesson once and carry a tote for the rest of your life.

Download Your Apps Before You Land

Uber, Cabify, Bolt, Google Translate, the Metro Madrid app, and WhatsApp. All of it, set up and ready before your wheels touch the tarmac. There is a full post on exactly which apps to download and why. Future you will be grateful that present you handled this.

Seatbelts In The Back Seat Are Mandatory

In Spain wearing a seatbelt is mandatory for all passengers in every seat including the back seat. This is strictly enforced and fines apply. Whether you are in a taxi, a Cabify, a Bolt, or anyone else's car, buckle up the moment you get in.

Google Is Not Always Your Friend Here

Specifically when it comes to holiday closures. There is an entire post dedicated to this topic because learning it the hard way is not the experience you want. Google Maps will confidently tell you a place is open when it is completely, absolutely not open. Plan accordingly. We will get into all of it.

What Other Guides Do Not Tell You

The food schedule will genuinely catch you off guard Lunch at 2 PM. Dinner at 9 PM or later. This is not a suggestion. This is how the city runs. Plan around it and you will eat extraordinarily well.
Para llevar is not always an option Not every restaurant offers takeaway. Ask before you order. The phrase is para llevar.
The hills are real Parts of historic Madrid involve significant uphill walking. Comfortable shoes are not optional.
The metro closes at 1:30 AM After that you need the Airport Express Bus, a taxi, or a rideshare. Plan your late nights accordingly.
Semana Santa will close the city and Google will not tell you Holy Week closures are widespread and poorly reflected in online listings. Check esmadrid.com before any important outing around Easter.
Seatbelts apply in the back seat Mandatory for all passengers in every seat. Fines apply.
The grocery store has rules Put on the plastic gloves before touching fruit and vegetables. This is standard practice and expected of everyone.
Tap water is safe and free Madrid's tap water is excellent. Ask for agua del grifo at restaurants to avoid being charged for bottled water.
WhatsApp is how Spain communicates Set it up before you land. Many businesses, landlords, and locals communicate exclusively through WhatsApp.
