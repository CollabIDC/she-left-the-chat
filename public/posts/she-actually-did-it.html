I have wanted to live abroad for forty years.

Not visit. Not vacation. Live. Wake up somewhere else and call it home. That dream has been with me longer than most of the people in my life. And for forty years, I kept walking past it.

Not because I forgot about it. Because someone always needed something first.

That's the thing nobody talks about when they tell the story of a dream deferred. It's rarely one big dramatic moment where you choose wrong. It's quieter than that. It's a thousand small moments where someone had a need, and you filled it. Automatically. Before you even asked yourself if you had anything left to give.

I raised three kids. Two of them weren't biologically mine. I loved all three of them like I had given birth to every single one. And I don't regret a moment of that love. But love was never the problem.

The problem was a pattern I didn't even know I had inherited.

My grandmother did it. She put my grandfather first even after he divorced her. My mother did it. She built her whole life around everyone else's needs and called it devotion. And I did it too. Not because anyone forced me. Because it was the water I grew up swimming in. You absorb what you see. You repeat what was modeled. And nobody ever stops to say, wait, whose life is this actually?

There's a word for what we were doing. Actually there are three.

Self-abandonment. Self-erasure. Dutiful disappearing.

And the cost showed up the same way every time. Lack. Financial lack because I was always stabilizing someone else. Emotional lack because I was always available for everyone else's life. And a quiet, persistent lack of self because I had been last in my own story for so long I forgot I was even in it.

The dream of living abroad didn't die during those forty years. It just waited. In the back of the room. Patient in a way I never was for myself.

*   *   *

Then one October, everything changed.

I did the work. The scripture says faith without works is dead, and I took that seriously. I signed with a relocation company. I hired an immigration lawyer through their package. I handled every detail, every form, every deadline. And I made sure Chloe was coming with me because she was never optional. The $740 in fees for her health certificate and USDA endorsement was paid before I had confirmed anything else, because some decisions aren't actually decisions. She was coming. Full stop.

I also booked a Delta One flight for 420,000 miles because if I was finally doing this after forty years, I was doing it right.

And quietly, methodically, in the background of all of it, I had been building a nest egg for years. Because I am a program manager. Planning is not something I do. It is something I am. I had a plan B in place long before I ever booked that flight. I was not about to land in another country and put stress on anyone's system. I had saved intentionally, responsibly, specifically for this. Just in case.

I just didn't know how soon just in case was going to show up.

My eldest daughter had been in my corner through all of it. She already lives abroad. She knew what was possible because she was already living it. From the very beginning of the process she kept saying go. Keep going. You can do this. When the visa came through she told me she was proud of me. Not surprised. Proud. Like she always knew this moment was coming.

And then my client contract fell through.

No income. No safety net visible to anyone on the outside. Just everything I had already set in motion, a nest egg that was about to go from plan B to plan A a lot faster than expected, and a decision about whether I actually believed what I always said I believed.

I called my daughter. And she didn't hesitate. She said go anyway. This one is for you. No more sacrificing for everyone else. It's your turn.

People say they are stepping out on faith. But most of the time they have something to fall back on. A backup plan. A person. A safety net just out of frame. I only had my faith.

So I prayed on it. And then I stood still and let the Lord work. The timing aligned. Everything that needed to fall into place did.

Good thing I'm a planner. Because faith moves mountains and spreadsheets keep you fed while it's working.

And I got on the plane.

*   *   *

I'm telling you this because I know there is someone reading this right now who has their own forty year dream sitting in the back of a room somewhere. And they keep walking past it. Not because they don't want it. Because someone always needed something first.

This is for that person.

It is not too late. The dream didn't move on without you. And you don't need a perfect plan or a fully funded account or the right moment or anyone's permission.

Sometimes you only have your faith.

And sometimes, if you're a program manager who has never met a contingency she didn't plan for, you also have a spreadsheet.

Both will get you there.
